For a lot of long-term diabetic patients, the feet are the first place
things go quietly wrong. Nerves lose sensitivity, small cuts don't
heal, and by the time anyone notices, an infection is already well
under way. Once a month we run a short evening session at the clinic
for patients and their families — no slides, no jargon, just the
practical stuff.

## What we covered

- **Why diabetic feet are different** — how high blood sugar over the
  years affects circulation and the nerves that let you feel a stone
  in your slipper.
- **The daily check** — a two-minute routine anyone can do at home,
  or a family member can do for an elderly patient.
- **What we look for in the clinic** — monofilament sensitivity
  testing, biothesiometry, pulse checks, and skin inspection.
- **Footwear and small injuries** — practical rules for shoes,
  socks, hot water, nail care, and what to do the moment you notice
  a cut, blister, or crack.

## Why it matters

Foot problems are one of the leading reasons long-term diabetics end
up hospitalised — and almost all of that risk is avoidable with an
early, consistent routine. The people who do best are almost always
the ones whose spouse or child has agreed to look at their feet once
a week and flag anything new.

> The best foot exam is the one your family does on a Sunday evening,
> not the one we do once a year.
